"I suppose when you're used to go at light speed, going at 120mph does feel slow."

My friend said this so casually and probably hasn't thought about it a minute since but it has echoed in my mind ever since. 

I'd been mentioning that I was doing well in my classes, getting A's, but that it was just taking *so much time* and I felt so slow. I had spent the day running through one section of one module for Network+ because the concept of subnet masks was simply eluding me.

It came down to math. I simply need to learn the math. Now it feels like a breeze.

But when I have an average of fifteen sections to get through in a week, one a day just isn't a realistic schedule. So I was being hard on myself for that fact rather than reflecting on what I was actually managing to succeed in.

After all, this week I changed out the RAM in my computer. Four months ago I would have looked at the situation, asked my brother in law how to deal with it, realized it would require somebody to physically do something and he's in another state, and either lived with the issues or bought a whole new computer that I can't afford.

Four. Months.

I'm still gasted in the flabbers every time I think about it. I used to listen to people talk about computer problems with a "ah man, that sucks" approach. Maybe a quick Google to see if it was a common bug. Now I'm looking at every technical situation with an eye for diagnosis. What is actually wrong and how can it be fixed?

I take one IT class and suddenly it's my whole personality.

## The Origin Story

About a year ago, I wanted a very specific tool for writing inspiration. My brain thrives on novelty (look at the reason for Abuddy for example) so I wanted prompts that I didn't necessarily come up with myself. I also didn't want it to be just a plain list that I had to be diligent about - that quickly becomes unnovel. What's stopping me from picking something else on the list?

Nothing I could find online was fitting the bill. They were too generic, too repetetive, too boring, too off putting, etc etc etc. More than that, they also were not geared toward the project that I wanted to write -- of course not, they were made to appeal to the most amount of people.

I decided not to let that stop me. At first I was looking at ways to make Excel randomly generate output but nobody seemed to have a decent tutorial for that. Instead, I found Perchance. It served my needs really well: make a list of things, tell the output to pull from that list, hit randomize, receive prompt from list.

Neat, uncomplicated, really simple to set up and use.

But suddenly, now that I had the tool and knew the basics I wanted to see what else I could do with it. I was creating multiple lists for output, creating if then functions, learning how to keep a prompt from appearing when the page is first loaded, learning how to create a quick copy prompt button, how to allows custom inputs for lists by users, and diving into more and more tutorials.

Around the same time, it was becoming clear that my job was going to fall apart sometime soon and I needed a pivot. I signed up for school thinking that I'd go part-time while working.

That didn't happen.
